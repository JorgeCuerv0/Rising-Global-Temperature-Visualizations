Introduction

The causes, effects and dangers of climate change are well-known in the academic community. Scientists first established a link between atmospheric Carbon Dioxide (CO2) and global temperature in 1967, and have known about rising CO2 levels since at least 1976. Analysis continues to this day as researchers race to understand the exact causes, effects, and risks of climate change.

It is critical for leaders and citizens to understand how climate change is progressing the U.S., the damage it has already caused, and the incredible danger it poses in the future. This website highlights the relationships and risk areas of U.S. temperature increases, natural disasters, and the costs of these disasters.

Temperatures are Increasing Across the United States

The average nationwide temperature has increased by 3 degrees since 1960. Almost every state has seen its own increase.

Scientists understand that a mere 1 to 2 degrees of warming can have a substantial impact on the rate of natural disasters, biodiversity, sea levels, and human health.

Use the 'State' filter boxes, or hover over the graph, to explore how temperature has changed for individual states since 1960.

Temperature Increases are Correlated with U.S. Natural Disasters

Natural disasters can grow more frequent or intense as a result of climate change. We observe that states experiencing a greater temperature change also experience more disasters per square mile, especially for fires and droughts.

Disasters by State, Type, and Year

Disasters can be plotted at the level of states and counties to understand which geographies are most impacted.

Use the 'Type' and 'Year(s)' dropdown menus above the maps to explore how the U.S. was affected by different types of disasters for the past 20 years.

Disasters by County

FEMA data documents the name, location, and type of each disaster declared within the past 20 years.

Use the 'Category', State(s) and 'Year(s)' dropdown menus to explore lists of specific disasters in the United States over time. Click on the column labels in the list to learn which counties are most heavily affected by specific types of disasters.

Disaster Intensity by Square Mileage

Some states may have more natural disasters within their borders simply due to being larger. "Intensity" - a normalized measurement which is the number of disasters divided by square mileage - shows that certain states, like California, are vulnerable to natural disasters even when adjusting for their size.

Use the dropdown menus to explore how intensity varies for certain types of natural disasters and certain years.
